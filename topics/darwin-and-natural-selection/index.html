Topic 2: Darwin and Natural Selection

Reading

  1. Darwin, Charles. 1859. The Origin of Species. First edition (of 6). Read Chapter 3. Then, read just up to page 10 in Chapter 4, and finally the final paragraph of Chapter 14 (on the last page). Available via Blackboard.
  2. Waters, C. Kenneth. 2003. “The Arguments in the Origin of Species.” In  The Cambridge Companion to Darwin, edited by Gregory Radick and Jonathan Hodge, 116–139. Cambridge University Press. via Blackboard. The entire book is also available at Hofstra Axinn Library: QH31.D2 C185 2003.
  3. Lewontin, Richard C. 1978. “Adaptation.” Scientific American 239 (3): 212-231 (September 1978). via Hofstra Library here. Note that a number of the pages included here are full-page ads or illustrations.

Reading questions

Darwin

  1. What is “the struggle for existence,” and what different forms does it take?
  2. What are the causes of there being everywhere struggles for existence?
  3. When we look at nature and see abundance, what does Darwin insist we’re overlooking?
  4. What is the Staffordshire heath example supposed to demonstrate?
  5. What is the clover/humble-bee example supposed to demonstrate?

Waters

  1. What is the core of Herschel’s philosophy of science and how could it structure Darwin’s argument?
  2. What is Ruse’s alternative reading of Darwin’s argument, based on a different philosophy of science, and why does Waters reject it?
  3. Since “many if not most nineteenth- and early twentieth-century evolutionists remained highly sceptical of natural selection,” how did Darwin address his argument to such doubters?
  4. How, in some of the arguments in Origin, is natural selection not an essential part of Darwin’s view?
  5. What two different ideas are involved in the idea of the tree of life? (see 119)
  6. “When Darwin invoked natural selection to explain a difficult case of evolution,” what reasoning pattern did he use over and over?
